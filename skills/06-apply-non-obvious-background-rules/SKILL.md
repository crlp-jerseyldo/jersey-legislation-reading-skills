# Purpose

Help the reader notice background rules or interpretive features that may matter even where the provision does not expressly point to them.

# Use When

- the text appears incomplete if read literally in isolation
- general interpretive rules may affect tense, number, power, procedure, or commencement
- there is reason to think a broader legal reading framework matters

# Do Not Use For

- broad theorising detached from the text
- assuming a background rule definitely resolves the issue
- replacing source-backed legal research

# Inputs

- provision reading so far
- unresolved interpretive issues
- any known general interpretive context relevant to Jersey legislation

# Method

1. Identify the narrow issue that might require a background rule.
2. State the possible background rule category at a high level.
3. Explain how that rule might matter to reading.
4. Distinguish clearly between a reading aid and a concluded legal answer.
5. If the point materially affects outcome, recommend escalation or further checking.

# Output Format

- Issue noticed:
- Possible background rule:
- Why it may matter:
- Confidence level:
- Need for further checking:

# Common Traps

- overstating a background rule as if it automatically decides the case
- introducing background material with no concrete trigger
- ignoring commencement, scope, or procedural context
- drifting from reading aid into unsupported legal conclusion

# Dependency Notes

This skill should be used sparingly. It is usually a bridge to boundary-checking, not an endpoint.
